Background

The Quantitative Turtle Analysis Project (QTAP) was created in 2019 with the goal of investigating how machine learning can be used to study wildlife populations using capture-recapture methods. Specifically, QTAP investigates how digital images of the eastern box turtle (Terrapene carolina carolina) can be used by automated programs to recognize unique individual turtles in place of marking systems like scute-notching, which is the current marking protocol. For the past four years, the QTAP field protocol has involved eastern box turtle field searches in two refuge plots, although some surveys extend outside the defined borders (Figure 1). Measurements and identifying photographs are taken for each turtle on each capture occasion. This protocol, combined with community science photo reports, has provided QTAP with over 500 turtle carapace photos and contributes meaningful data for capture-recapture analysis for monitoring the eastern box turtle populations at the Patuxent Wildlife Refuge. This project builds upon decades of eastern box turtle research initiated by former Patuxent Wildlife Research Center director, Lucille Farrier Stickel, in the 1940s, contributing to long-term monitoring and analysis of population changes over time. (Stickel, 1950).

Research

The 2022 QTAP field effort was supported by three technicians this summer. Will Heinle received an Arts and Sciences Research Fellowship and Noelle Beswick was supported by the Spider Internship Fund, both through the University of Richmond. Emily Wapman was hired through the ESA/USGS Cooperative Summer Field Training Program, a collaborative program with the Ecological Society of America and USGS to place undergraduate students in USGS research labs with assigned mentors. Will and Noelle led the field effort, data management, and detection study for the first half of the summer and Emily continued field surveys and data analysis through September. The QTAP field effort was also supported by the Youth Conservation Corps (YCC) and by high school student, Auden. These students took part in field surveys and observed the morphometric data collection process.

The QTAP team finished the survey season with 229 surveys and a total of 114 live captures through search and 12 live captures during the detection study. 86 were unique individuals, and 41 were new captures. Notable Events

In 2017, a transmitter was attached to the carapace of turtle 220 for tracking via telemetry. However, at some point after attachment, this transmitter lost function. Turtle 220 was recaptured in 2020 before the field team knew of the transmitter’s loss of function. Over the last two years, an effort was made to search for turtle 220 and remove the failed transmitter, but she could not be found. On September 12, 2022, turtle 220 was relocated during a search in the cabin detection plot. She was brought to the main office for transmitter removal and Glenn, a wildlife veterinarian on site, was ultimately successful in prying off the transmitter. Turtle 220 was returned to the location she was found in the cabin plot.

Citizen Science

For the past four years, QTAP has requested community science reports of eastern box turtles found on EESC or the public refuge. Reporters are asked to provide GPS coordinates of location, photographs, date, and time. We received 122 community science reports this year, 20 of which were notched turtles with confirmed identities. Citizen sighting were reported from North, Central, and South tracts of the refuge by both citizens and refuge staff members. These reports are useful for studying eastern box turtle presence and movements in refuge areas outside standard survey plots and will be used in capture-recapture analysis for refuge population estimates.

Detection Study

The detection study was a new project added to the QTAP in 2022. Eastern box turtles can be difficult to spot due to their camouflage with the forest floor. The team sought to investigate the detection probability of experienced surveyors to better understand survey error. Three plots were established: powerline, cabin, and triangle. Empty box turtle shells, donated by collaborators at Towson University, were placed by Will or Noelle at randomly generated coordinates within each of the plots. Approximately half of the shells in each plot were partially covered (left image) and half were exposed (right image).

Surveyors, unaware of shell locations, walked throughout the plot, covering the area as thoroughly as possible looking for the hidden shells. If a shell was spotted, surveyors measured their distance to the shell and took photos and GPS coordinates of the shell location. This study took place for five weeks with surveys primarily done by Andy, Emily, Will, and Noelle, although several volunteers took part in the study during the first week.
